## The Announcement

**The Story:**

- **December 9, 2018**: Gerald Cotten dies in India
- **Cause**: Complications from Crohn's disease
- **Age**: 30 years old
- **Problem**: He had **sole access** to exchange's cold wallets
- **Locked**: $190M+ in customer crypto (BTC, ETH, LTC, etc.)
- **Claim**: "Passwords died with him"

**The Shock:**

- **Canada's biggest exchange**
- **115,000 customers** affected
- **$190 million** inaccessible
- **Sole access**: Massive red flag
- **Suspicious timing**: Right before major withdrawals

**Immediate Questions:**

> "How does an exchange not have backup access?"

## Gerald Cotten

## The Unraveling

**Initial Investigation (January-February 2019):**

**Ernst & Young** (bankruptcy trustee) discovered:

1. **Cold wallets mostly empty**: Only ~$30M found
2. **$190M shortfall**: Most funds missing
3. **Fake trades**: Gerald trading against himself
4. **Ponzi dynamics**: Using new deposits to pay old withdrawals
5. **Personal spending**: Transferred funds to himself

**The Bombshell:**

Those "secure cold wallets with lost passwords"?

**They were empty.** Gerald had withdrawn the funds **years ago**.

## The Canadian Regulatory Failure

**How Did This Happen?**

**Canadian regulations (2013-2019):**

- **Exchanges unregulated**: No licensing requirement
- **No oversight**: FINTRAC rules weak
- **No audits**: Voluntary only
- **No insurance**: Customer funds unprotected
- **No segregation**: Could commingle funds

**After QuadrigaCX:**

- **Regulations tightened**: Exchange licensing required
- **Audits mandatory**: Third-party verification
- **Customer fund rules**: Segregation required
- **Insurance encouraged**: But not mandatory

**The Irony:**

Canada had **more regulations** than most countries, but they were **poorly enforced**.
