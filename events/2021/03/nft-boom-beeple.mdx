On March 11, 2021, digital artist **Mike Winkelmann (Beeple)** sells "Everydays: The First 5000 Days" as an NFT for **$69.3 million** at Christie's auction house, instantly becoming the third-most expensive artwork sold by a living artist.

## The Artwork

_"Everydays: The First 5000 Days"_ is a digital collage of 5,000 individual images that Beeple created daily from May 1, 2007, to January 7, 2021. The 316 MB JPG file represents over 13 years of continuous artistic practice.

**Auction Details:**

- **Opening bid**: $100
- **Final price**: $69,346,250
- **Buyer**: Vignesh Sundaresan (MetaKovan)
- **Format**: ERC-721 NFT on Ethereum
- **Auction house**: Christie's (first major NFT sale)

## Cultural Earthquake

This sale fundamentally changed how people viewed digital art and ownership:

**Before Beeple Sale:**

- NFTs mostly limited to crypto-native collectors
- Total NFT market: ~$100M annually
- Skepticism about "paying for JPEGs"

**After Beeple Sale:**

- Mainstream media explosion
- Traditional auction houses embrace NFTs
- Celebrity and brand participation
- **Q1 2021 NFT sales**: $2 billion

## The Technology

NFTs proved blockchain's utility beyond finance:

- **Provable ownership** of digital items
- **Programmable royalties** (creators earn from secondary sales)
- **Composability** (use your NFT across platforms)
- **Community access** (token-gated experiences)

## Criticism and Reality

The NFT boom attracted significant criticism:

- Environmental concerns (before Ethereum's Merge)
- Speculation and wash trading
- Right-click save debates
- Scams and rug pulls
- Unclear copyright implications

Despite the bubble dynamics, NFTs demonstrated real innovation in digital ownership, creator economics, and community building that continues evolving beyond the 2021 hype cycle.
